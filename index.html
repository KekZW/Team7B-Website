Background

The River Clyde is one of Scotland‚Äôs most significant rivers, flowing for about 170 kilometers from its source in the Lowther Hills through Glasgow and into the Firth of Clyde. Historically, the Clyde played a crucial role in Scotland‚Äôs industrial growth during the 18th and 19th centuries, becoming a world-renowned center for shipbuilding and maritime trade. For local communities, the Clyde has long been a source of livelihood, identity, and pride. Its banks supported thriving industries, provided jobs, and shaped Glasgow into a major economic hub. Today, while its industrial role has declined, the river remains important to locals for recreation, tourism, conservation, and regeneration projects. Efforts to clean and revitalize the Clyde have also restored wildlife habitats and public access, reinforcing its cultural and environmental value to the people of Scotland.

Problem Statement

When conducting activities by the river, the condition of the river greatly impacts safety aspects. The Scottish Environment Protection Agency (SEPA) does publish data around river flow, but it is difficult to decipher‚Äîespecially when it comes to predicting river conditions ahead of time. Another aspect is that the river flow rate upstream affects pollution levels downstream, but again, this is difficult to derive from the available data.

Key Points

River condition data can be difficult to understand, especially for non-technical users.

There is a lack of accessible tools to help predict future river conditions based on current and upstream data.
